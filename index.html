The operating principle

Validate the wrappers,
not the brain.

Bringing AI into clinical drug development is blocked by trust: regulators require every number to be traceable, reproducible, independently checked, and human-signed-off. Naïve “let the LLM do it” fails that bar.

My work treats the model as a fast, qualified author — but lets nothing ship until deterministic, independent, auditable gates pass and a named human approves. It’s the bridge that lets modern AI’s speed reach a place where a wrong number is unacceptable.

Study-data chatbot

Ask a trial’s data anything — and it structurally cannot make a number up.

Why it matters

Non-technical staff wait on programmers for every ad-hoc question, and generic “chat with your data” tools hallucinate numbers — a non-starter in a trial.

What it does

Plain-English Q&A over a study’s SDTM/ADaM data. The model never computes a number — it writes audited, read-only SQL, the database runs it, and it narrates the real rows. Every answer is watermarked DRAFT with a visible query trace and a one-click hand-off into the formal double-programming QC pipeline; KM and waterfall charts render inline.

SDTM → ADaM pipeline + QC gate

Two independent builds, one deterministic gate — trust that comes from proof, not from trusting the model.

Why it matters

Regulatory programming rests on independent double-programming QC: two people build the same dataset separately and a tool proves they match. It’s the core trust mechanism — and it’s expensive and manual.

What it does

The full regulated lifecycle — raw → SDTM → ADaM → define.xml — with spec-before-code, an AI author, and an independent QC programmer architecturally blocked from reading the production code, reconciled by a deterministic PROC COMPARE gate that must hit zero differences before anything ships.

About

I’m a statistician and statistical programmer — a PhD in Statistics & Data Science and roughly five years in SAS/CDISC clinical programming. My doctoral research developed a Bayesian zero-inflated, unipolar mixture item-response-theory model with covariate-dependent latent classification, applied to patient-reported symptom data — Bayesian hierarchical modeling, latent-class structure, and questionnaire/PRO measurement.

Over the last year I designed and built a “virtual biometrics department” — a system of AI agents that produce and independently QC SDTM/ADaM datasets and analysis documents for clinical trials. Its guiding principle is “validate the wrappers, not the brain”: the LLM is a qualified author whose output can’t leave the system until deterministic, independently checkable gates pass and a named human signs off. That posture is what lets modern AI touch a regulated, high-stakes domain at all — and it’s the thread through all three projects above.
